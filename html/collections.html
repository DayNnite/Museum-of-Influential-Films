Museum of Influential Films

Archaeology — The Early Visionaries

A Trip to the Moon (1902)

Georges Méliès' hand-painted spectacle showed how imagination could transform film from documentation into art.

The Jazz Singer (1927)

The first feature-length “talkie” ended the silent era and forever changed how stories were told on screen.

Metropolis (1927)

Fritz Lang’s dystopian epic pioneered special effects and visual design that still inspire science-fiction films today.

Anthropology — Film as Reflection

Do the Right Thing (1989)

Spike Lee’s vibrant portrait of one Brooklyn block captured the heat and tension of American race relations.

Spirited Away (2001)

Hayao Miyazaki’s Oscar-winning masterpiece blended folklore, identity, and environmental themes into one timeless story.

Black Panther (2018)

Ryan Coogler’s Afrofuturist vision redefined representation and global box-office expectations for superhero films.

History — Modern Classics

Star Wars (1977)

George Lucas combined mythic storytelling and cutting-edge effects to launch the blockbuster era.

Titanic (1997)

James Cameron merged epic romance and technical innovation, creating one of the most successful films in history.

The Matrix (1999)

The Wachowskis fused cyberpunk philosophy and revolutionary effects, altering how action and reality were portrayed.

Parasite (2019)

Bong Joon-ho’s genre-bending masterpiece made history as the first non-English Best Picture winner.
